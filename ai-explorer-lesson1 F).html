What Is AI?

📖 Read Together

The Story of Zip the Robot 🤖

Once upon a time, there was a robot named Zip. Zip wasn't like robots in movies — Zip couldn't walk around or talk or feel happy or sad. Zip could only do one thing: look at pictures and guess what was in them.

Zip's friends wanted to teach Zip what a dog looked like. So they showed Zip thousands and thousands of pictures. Every time Zip saw a dog, they said "Yes, that's a dog!" Every time it wasn't a dog, they said "Nope, try again!"

After seeing SO many pictures, something amazing happened. Zip got really, really good at spotting dogs — even dogs Zip had never seen before!

But here's the funny part. Zip didn't know what a dog felt like, or that dogs could be your best friend, or that some dogs are scared of thunderstorms. Zip just knew what a dog looked like in a picture.

That's AI! It's really good at finding patterns — but it doesn't understand things the way you do.

🎮 Activity: Be the AI!

The Pattern Game

Now it's your turn to think like an AI! This game is called "What's My Rule?"

💡 Example rules to get you started:

🔴 Things that are red  ·  🔵 Things that are round  ·  🍎 Things you can eat
🐾 Animals  ·  📦 Things that fit in a box  ·  💧 Things that are wet
🌙 Things you use at night  ·  🔊 Things that make noise

  1. 1
    One person is the Rule Keeper — they pick a secret rule from the examples above, or make up their own!
  2. 2
    The other person is the AI — they look around the room and point to different things, one at a time.
  3. 3
    The Rule Keeper only says "Yes!" or "Nope!" — no other hints!
  4. 4
    The AI keeps guessing based on the pattern of yes/no answers until they figure out the secret rule.
  5. 5
    Switch roles and play again! 🎉

💡 After the game, talk about it: How did you figure out the rule? You found the pattern — just like AI does!

🌟 Can you think of something in real life that uses pattern-finding? (Hint: think about your favorite apps or games!)

🧠 AI learns from examples, not from understanding — and that changes everything.

📖 Read Together

How Does AI Actually Learn? 🧠

Here's a question: how did you learn what a chair is?

Nobody gave you a rulebook that said "four legs + flat seat + back support = chair." You just saw chairs — lots of different ones — and eventually your brain figured it out. A beanbag is kind of a chair. A tree stump can be a chair. A shopping cart is definitely not a chair.

AI learns almost the same way. You show it millions of examples. You tell it which ones are correct. It adjusts. You repeat. After enough examples, it gets really good at recognizing things it's never seen before.

This is called Machine Learning — and it powers almost every piece of AI you've ever used. Your Netflix recommendations. Snapchat filters. Google Translate. Spam filters in email. All of them learned from examples.

But here's the crucial difference: you understand what a chair is. AI only recognizes what a chair looks like. It has no idea what it feels like to sit in one, that chairs are for resting, or that sometimes you spin in them just for fun.

Understanding vs. pattern recognition. That's the line between human intelligence and artificial intelligence.

🎮 Activity: Human Algorithm

Train Your Own AI

This activity shows you exactly how machine learning works — using your brain as the model.

💡 Example rules to choose from:

🌱 Things that are alive  ·  🔊 Things that make noise  ·  🍳 Things in a kitchen
✈️ Things that can fly  ·  📚 Things used at school  ·  🌊 Things found near water
🔤 Things with the letter "A" in their name  ·  🟡 Things that are yellow

  1. 1
    One person is the Algorithm — they secretly choose a rule from the examples above, or invent a tricky one of their own!
  2. 2
    The other person is the Trainer — they name or point to objects. The Algorithm only responds "In" or "Out." No other clues.
  3. 3
    The Trainer keeps a mental note of what's In and Out — looking for the pattern. That mental note is your training data.
  4. 4
    When the Trainer thinks they know the rule, they make a prediction about a new object they haven't tried yet. Were they right?
  5. 5
    Challenge round: Try a tricky rule where the pattern isn't obvious — like "things with the letter A in their name." See how many examples it takes to crack it!

💡 The debrief: What you just experienced — collecting examples, finding patterns, making predictions — is literally what happens inside AI. Millions of times per second.

💬 Talk About It

Go deeper with these questions

📱 What apps or technology do you use that you think might be using AI? How would it have learned to do what it does?
🤖 If AI can only learn from examples, what happens if the examples are wrong or unfair? Can you think of a way that could cause a problem?
🧠 What's something you understand that you think would be nearly impossible to teach an AI just from examples?
